Time line of Witold Pilecki's life:

Early Life

  • May 13, 1901: Born in Olonets, Russian Empire (now part of Russia).
  • 1910: Moved with his family to Wilno (Vilnius), a city with strong Polish cultural influence.
  • 1914-1918: Attended the gymnasium in Wilno. Active in the Polish underground scouting movement.
  • 1918-1919: Joined the Polish Self-Defense Forces of Wilno, fighting against Lithuanian and Bolshevik forces.
  • 1920: Participated in the Battle of Warsaw during the Polish–Soviet War.

Interwar Years

  • 1921-1922: Studied at the University of Poznań, Faculty of Agriculture.
  • 1922-1924: Continued studies at Stefan Batory University, Faculty of Fine Arts
  • 1926: Joined the Polish Army's 26th Infantry Regiment in Lida.
  • 1931: Married Maria Ostrowska; they had two children, Andrzej and Zofia.
  • 1932-1937: Commanded the 1st Lida Military Training Squadron. Received numerous military decorations.
  • 1938: Joined the army reserve as a cavalry officer.

World War II

  • September 1939: Mobilized during the German invasion of Poland. Defended Warsaw with the 41st Infantry Division.
  • October 1939: Co-founded the Secret Polish Army (Tajna Armia Polska, TAP), a resistance movement.
  • September 1940: Pilecki volunteered to be imprisoned in Auschwitz to gather intelligence on the camp's operations and Nazi atrocities. He was arrested by the Gestapo and sent to Auschwitz under the alias "Tomasz Serafiński."
  • 1940-1943: Inside Auschwitz, Pilecki formed the Związek Organizacji Wojskowej (ZOW), an underground resistance organization. ZOW gathered information, documented Nazi crimes, and smuggled reports out of the camp to the Polish resistance2.
  • 1940-1943: Pilecki meticulously documented the horrors of Auschwitz, including the mass murders, inhumane conditions, and brutal treatment of prisoners. His reports were among the first to reveal the extent of Nazi atrocities to the outside world1.
  • April 1943: Pilecki orchestrated a daring escape from Auschwitz. After escaping, he wrote a comprehensive report on the camp's conditions and operations, which was smuggled to the Polish government-in-exile and the Allies.
  • August 1944: Pilecki joined the Warsaw Uprising, fighting against the German occupation forces as part of the Home Army (Armia Krajowa). His bravery and leadership were crucial in the resistance efforts.
  • October 1944: Taken as a prisoner of war and sent to Lamsdorf and Murnau POW camps in Germany.

After the War

  • 1945: Released from POW camp by Allied forces. Returned to Poland and continued working for the Polish resistance against the Communist regime.
  • 1946: Operated clandestinely under the alias "Roman Jezierski" while gathering intelligence on Soviet activities in Poland.
  • May 8, 1947: Arrested by the Communist authorities in Poland.
  • March 1948: Subjected to a show trial, accused of espionage and planning an anti-communist uprising.
  • May 25, 1948: Executed in Mokotów Prison, Warsaw.

Legacy

  • Post-1989: After the fall of Communism in Poland, Pilecki's story became widely recognized and celebrated.
  • 1990s: Posthumously awarded the Order of Polonia Restituta and the Order of the White Eagle.
  • 2006: Recognized by the Polish government as one of the greatest heroes of the Polish nation.
  • Memorials: Numerous memorials, schools, and streets in Poland are named in his honor.
